How to Finance Your First Fix & Flip

March 12, 2026 7 min read

Flipping houses sounds exciting — buy low, renovate, sell high. The HGTV version makes it look effortless. But the financing is where most first-timers get tripped up, and it's often the difference between a profitable flip and a costly mistake.

If you're planning your first flip, understanding how the money works isn't optional. It's the foundation everything else is built on. Here's what you need to know.

Why Banks Won't Fund Your Flip

Before we get into what works, let's talk about what doesn't: traditional banks.

Most first-time flippers assume they'll walk into their local bank, explain the deal, and walk out with a loan. It almost never works that way. Here's why:

  • Banks don't like short-term loans. They make money on 30-year mortgages. A 6-month loan on a distressed property doesn't fit their model.
  • Banks don't lend on distressed properties. If the house needs a new roof, has mold, or is missing a kitchen, it won't pass the bank's appraisal requirements.
  • Banks move too slowly. Traditional underwriting takes 30 to 60 days. In competitive markets, the deal will be gone by then.
  • Banks want income documentation. Tax returns, pay stubs, two years of employment history — none of which reflects your ability to execute a successful flip.

This isn't a criticism of banks. They're built for a different purpose. But for fix-and-flip investing, you need a different tool.

Enter Fix & Flip Loans

A fix & flip loan (sometimes called a rehab loan or bridge loan) is a short-term, asset-based loan designed specifically for buying and renovating investment properties.

Here's how it works at a high level:

  • One loan covers both the purchase and the renovation. You don't need separate financing for acquisition and rehab — it's all rolled into one package.
  • Terms are short: typically 6 to 12 months, with extensions available if needed.
  • Payments are interest-only. You're not paying down principal during the project. This keeps your monthly carrying costs low while you're renovating.
  • Rates are higher than a traditional mortgage (typically 9% to 13%), but you're only paying that rate for a few months — not 30 years.
  • Qualification is deal-based. The lender cares primarily about the property, the numbers, and your plan. Not your W-2.

The loan is structured around two key numbers: the purchase price (what you're buying the property for) and the rehab budget (what it will cost to renovate). Most lenders will finance 80% to 90% of the purchase price and up to 100% of the rehab costs, depending on the deal and your experience level.

How Draws Work

This is the part that catches most first-timers off guard: you don't get all the rehab money upfront.

Here's the typical process:

  1. At closing, the lender funds the purchase price portion. The rehab budget is held in reserve (sometimes called a "holdback").
  2. You begin renovations using your own funds or contractor credit for the first phase of work.
  3. When a phase is complete, you submit a draw request to the lender. This is a formal request to release a portion of the rehab funds.
  4. The lender sends an inspector to verify the work was actually completed and matches the original scope of work.
  5. Once verified, the lender releases the funds — either to you or directly to your contractor.
  6. Repeat for each phase of the renovation until the project is complete.

This draw process exists to protect the lender (and honestly, to protect you too). It ensures the money is actually being used for renovations and that the work is being done properly.

What this means for you: You need enough cash to fund the first phase of work out of pocket before you get reimbursed. Budget for this. If your rehab is $60K and you have four draws, you'll need roughly $15K available to float each phase.

What Lenders Look For

Every lender evaluates deals slightly differently, but here are the four factors that matter most:

1. The Deal Itself

This is the most important factor. Lenders want to see that the numbers make sense: a realistic purchase price, a reasonable rehab budget, and an ARV (after-repair value) that leaves enough margin. If the deal is strong, everything else becomes easier.

2. Your Experience

First-time flippers can absolutely get funded — but expect slightly more conservative terms. A lender might offer 85% of purchase to an experienced flipper and 80% to a first-timer on the same deal. As you build a track record, your terms improve.

3. Credit Score

Credit matters less than you think in private lending. Most lenders have a minimum around 620 to 660, but the deal itself carries far more weight. A borrower with a 680 credit score and a great deal will get funded before a borrower with an 800 score and a marginal deal.

4. Skin in the Game

Lenders want to see that you have money at risk. This usually means a down payment of 10% to 20% of the purchase price, plus closing costs. The more you bring to the table, the more confident the lender is that you're committed to executing the project.

The Numbers That Matter

Before you make an offer on a property, you need to understand these key metrics:

ARV (After-Repair Value)

What the property will be worth after renovations are complete. This is determined by comparable sales in the area — what similar, fully renovated homes have sold for recently. ARV is the single most important number in your deal. Everything flows from it.

LTV and LTC

Loan-to-Value (LTV) is the loan amount divided by the property's value. Loan-to-Cost (LTC) is the loan amount divided by the total project cost (purchase + rehab). Most fix & flip lenders will go up to 70% to 75% of ARV and 85% to 90% of the total cost — whichever is lower.

The 70% Rule

A widely used rule of thumb: don't pay more than 70% of ARV minus rehab costs. If a property's ARV is $300K and it needs $50K in work, the maximum you should offer is ($300K × 0.70) − $50K = $160K. This formula builds in enough margin for holding costs, selling costs, and profit.

Don't Forget Hidden Costs

Your profit margin needs to account for more than just purchase price and rehab. Factor in:

  • Holding costs: loan interest, insurance, property taxes, utilities during the renovation
  • Selling costs: real estate agent commissions (typically 5% to 6% of sale price), closing costs, transfer taxes
  • Loan costs: origination points (1 to 3 points), appraisal fees, inspection fees
  • The unexpected: budget overruns, delays, surprises behind the walls. Always build in a contingency of 10% to 15% of your rehab budget.

Tips for First-Timers

After working with hundreds of investors — from first-timers to seasoned pros — here's what we'd tell anyone doing their first flip:

Start with a Cosmetic Flip

Your first project should not be a gut rehab. Look for a property that needs paint, flooring, fixtures, landscaping, and maybe a kitchen and bath refresh — not structural work, foundation repair, or a full addition. Cosmetic flips are faster, cheaper, and far more forgiving of mistakes.

Get Multiple Contractor Bids

Never rely on a single estimate. Get at least three bids for any significant work. This helps you understand the real cost, spot outliers, and build relationships with reliable contractors. A good contractor is worth their weight in gold.

Build in Contingency

Things will go wrong. Plumbing issues behind the walls. A permit that takes longer than expected. Material prices that changed between your bid and your order. Add 10% to 15% to your rehab budget as a contingency — and don't touch it unless you have to.

Line Up Financing Before You Make an Offer

The worst time to figure out financing is after you've gone under contract. Talk to a broker, get pre-qualified, and understand your terms before you start making offers. This lets you move fast when the right deal appears — and move with confidence.

Know Your Exit Before You Enter

Before you buy, know exactly how you plan to sell. Who's your target buyer? What's the realistic sale price based on comps? How long will it take to sell in this market? Your exit strategy determines everything from your renovation scope to your hold time budget.
