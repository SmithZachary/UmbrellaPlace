Bridge Loan vs. DSCR: Which Is Right for Your Deal?

March 12, 2026 6 min read

You've found a deal. The numbers work. Now you need financing — and you're staring at two options that sound similar but serve very different purposes: bridge loans and DSCR loans.

Picking the wrong one won't just cost you money. It can kill a deal entirely. A bridge loan on a property you plan to hold for 10 years means you'll be forced to refinance under pressure. A DSCR loan on a flip means you're locked into a 30-year note on a property you plan to sell in four months.

Here's how to think about each option — and how to choose the right one for your specific deal.

What's a Bridge Loan?

A bridge loan is short-term financing designed to get you from point A to point B. It "bridges" the gap between acquiring a property and either selling it or refinancing into permanent debt.

Here's what defines a bridge loan:

  • Term length: 6 to 24 months, typically 12 months
  • Interest rates: 9% to 13%, depending on experience, leverage, and property type
  • Payments: Interest-only (you're not paying down principal)
  • Speed: Can close in 7 to 14 days — sometimes faster
  • Qualification: Primarily based on the deal itself, not your personal income or tax returns

Bridge loans are built for speed and flexibility. They exist because traditional banks can't (or won't) move fast enough for competitive real estate deals. When a seller wants to close in two weeks and the property needs $80K in renovations, a bridge loan is often the only realistic option.

Use a bridge loan when: you're buying to flip, you need to close fast to beat other offers, the property needs significant rehab, or you plan to refinance into a long-term loan after stabilizing the asset.

What's a DSCR Loan?

A DSCR (Debt Service Coverage Ratio) loan is long-term financing designed for rental properties. Instead of qualifying based on your personal income, it qualifies based on the property's rental income relative to its mortgage payment.

The core concept is simple: if the property's rent covers the mortgage (and then some), the loan is considered safe. A DSCR of 1.0 means rent exactly equals the payment. Most lenders want 1.0 or higher, and you'll get better terms at 1.25+.

Here's what defines a DSCR loan:

  • Term length: 30 years (with 5/1 ARM or fixed-rate options)
  • Interest rates: 6.5% to 9.5%, depending on DSCR ratio, credit, and leverage
  • Payments: Principal and interest (fully amortizing)
  • Speed: 21 to 30 days to close
  • Qualification: Based on rental income vs. mortgage payment — no tax returns or employment verification required

DSCR loans are the tool of choice for building a rental portfolio. Because they don't require personal income documentation, you can scale far beyond what conventional lending allows. Investor #1 with a W-2 showing $85K/year can own 15 rental properties if each one cash-flows.

Use a DSCR loan when: you're buying a rental property, you're refinancing a stabilized asset, you want 30-year fixed-rate debt, or your personal income doesn't reflect your true financial picture.

Which Should You Choose?

The answer depends entirely on your exit strategy — what you plan to do with the property after you buy it.

If you're flipping the property (buy, renovate, sell within 12 months), use a bridge loan. You need speed, you need rehab funds, and you'll pay off the loan when you sell. A 30-year DSCR loan makes no sense here — you'd be paying closing costs and potentially a prepayment penalty on a property you plan to own for a few months.

If you're buying a rent-ready property to hold, use a DSCR loan. You'll get a lower rate, a 30-year term, and stable monthly payments. There's no reason to use a bridge loan if the property doesn't need rehab and you don't need to close in under two weeks.

If you're buying a property that needs work, then plan to rent it out, you'll likely need both — and that's where the BRRRR strategy comes in.

The BRRRR Strategy: Using Both

Many experienced investors use bridge loans and DSCR loans together as part of the BRRRR strategy:

  1. Buy — Acquire a distressed property using a bridge loan (fast close, rehab funds included)
  2. Rehab — Renovate the property to increase its value and make it rent-ready
  3. Rent — Place a tenant and stabilize the rental income
  4. Refinance — Pay off the bridge loan by refinancing into a long-term DSCR loan based on the new appraised value
  5. Repeat — Use the cash pulled out during the refinance to fund your next deal

This strategy works because you're using each loan product for exactly what it was designed for. The bridge loan handles the acquisition and rehab phase where speed and flexibility matter. The DSCR loan handles the long-term hold phase where low rates and stable payments matter.

When executed well, BRRRR allows you to recycle the same capital across multiple deals — building a portfolio without needing fresh cash for every acquisition.
