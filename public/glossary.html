Lending Glossary

Plain-English definitions of common terms in private real estate lending.

After Repair Value (ARV)

The estimated market value of a property after all planned renovations and repairs are completed. Lenders use ARV to decide how much they're willing to lend on a fix-and-flip or value-add project. For example, if a home is worth $150,000 today but will be worth $250,000 after a full renovation, the ARV is $250,000.

Amortization

The process of spreading a loan's repayment over a series of fixed, scheduled payments that include both principal and interest. A fully amortizing loan is paid off completely by the end of the term. Many short-term real estate loans skip amortization entirely and use interest-only payments instead, with the full principal due at the end.

Appraisal

A professional, independent estimate of a property's current market value, performed by a licensed appraiser. Lenders typically require an appraisal before funding a loan to make sure the property is worth enough to serve as adequate collateral. Some private lenders may accept a broker price opinion (BPO) or internal valuation instead of a full appraisal.

Bridge Loan

A short-term loan used to "bridge" the gap between buying a property and securing longer-term financing or selling it. Bridge loans are popular for acquisitions that need to close quickly or for properties that don't yet qualify for conventional bank financing. Terms typically range from 6 to 24 months.

BRRRR Strategy

An investing strategy that stands for Buy, Rehab, Rent, Refinance, Repeat. The investor purchases a distressed property, renovates it, places a tenant, refinances into a long-term loan (like a DSCR loan), and then uses the freed-up capital to do it all over again. It's one of the most popular methods for building a rental portfolio with limited upfront cash.

Cap Rate

Short for capitalization rate, this is a quick measure of a rental property's return calculated by dividing its net operating income (NOI) by its purchase price or current value. For example, a property generating $12,000 per year in NOI with a value of $200,000 has a 6% cap rate. A higher cap rate generally suggests a higher potential return but may also indicate more risk.

Cash-Out Refinance

Replacing an existing mortgage with a new, larger loan and pocketing the difference in cash. Real estate investors often use a cash-out refinance to pull equity from a stabilized property so they can fund their next deal. This is the "Refinance" step in the BRRRR strategy.

Closing Costs

The fees and expenses paid when a real estate transaction is finalized, beyond the price of the property itself. These can include appraisal fees, title insurance, attorney fees, origination points, and recording charges. On a private real estate loan, closing costs typically run between 2% and 5% of the loan amount.

Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR)

A ratio that compares a property's rental income to its total debt payments (principal, interest, taxes, and insurance). A DSCR of 1.0 means income exactly equals debt payments, while most lenders look for 1.20 or higher. DSCR loans use this ratio to qualify borrowers based on the property's cash flow rather than personal income.

Draw / Draw Schedule

A system used in construction and renovation loans where funds are released in stages as work is completed, rather than all at once. Each draw is typically verified by an inspection to confirm the work meets the agreed-upon scope before the next portion of funds is disbursed. This protects the lender and keeps the project on track.

Fix & Flip

An investment strategy where you purchase a property in need of repair, renovate it, and sell it for a profit — ideally within a few months. Fix-and-flip loans are short-term loans specifically designed to fund both the purchase and the rehab costs of these projects. The lender typically bases the loan on the property's ARV rather than its current condition.

Hard Money Loan

A short-term loan secured primarily by the value of the real estate rather than the borrower's credit score or income. Hard money loans are funded by private investors or companies, close faster than bank loans, and are commonly used for fix-and-flip projects and bridge financing. The trade-off is typically a higher interest rate compared to conventional loans.

Interest-Only Payments

A payment structure where the borrower pays only the interest on the loan each month, without reducing the principal balance. This keeps monthly payments lower during the loan term, with the full principal due at maturity. Interest-only payments are the standard structure for most short-term investment loans like bridge and fix-and-flip financing.

Loan-to-Cost (LTC)

A ratio comparing the loan amount to the total cost of a project, including the purchase price and renovation budget. For example, if a project costs $200,000 total and the lender offers 85% LTC, you would receive a loan of $170,000. LTC is especially common in fix-and-flip and new construction lending.

Loan-to-Value (LTV)

A ratio comparing the loan amount to the appraised value (or purchase price) of a property. A lower LTV means the borrower has more equity — or "skin in the game" — which reduces risk for the lender and can lead to better rates and terms. Most private lenders cap LTV between 65% and 80%, depending on the loan type.

Origination Points

An upfront fee charged by the lender to process and fund a loan, expressed as a percentage of the loan amount. One "point" equals 1% of the loan — so on a $300,000 loan, two origination points would cost $6,000. Points are a standard part of private lending and are typically paid at closing.

Prepayment Penalty

A fee charged if you pay off a loan before the agreed-upon term ends. Not all loans carry a prepayment penalty, and the terms vary widely — some charge a percentage of the remaining balance while others require a minimum number of months of interest. Always ask about prepayment terms before signing so there are no surprises when you sell or refinance.

Private Lender / Private Money

An individual or non-bank company that funds real estate loans using their own capital or investor funds, rather than deposits like a traditional bank. Private lenders can often approve deals faster and with more flexible criteria, making them a popular choice for investment properties that don't fit the conventional lending box. Umbrella Place connects borrowers with a network of vetted private lenders to find the right fit for each deal.

Term Sheet

A document that outlines the key terms of a proposed loan — including the rate, points, term length, and conditions — before the full loan agreement is drafted. Think of it as a summary of the deal so both sides can agree on the big-picture details before moving forward with underwriting and closing.

Underwriting

The process a lender uses to evaluate the risk of a loan by reviewing the borrower's financials, the property's value, and the overall deal structure. In private lending, underwriting tends to focus more heavily on the property and the deal itself rather than the borrower's personal income or tax returns. A clean, well-documented deal package can speed up underwriting significantly.
